## 1. Introduction & Review Protocol

### 1.1 Research Topic

This systematic literature review (SLR) examines the interrelationships among **airline service quality (ASQ)**, **passenger satisfaction**, and **passenger loyalty** within the **low-cost carrier (LCC) segment**, with particular emphasis on the **Southeast Asian aviation market**. The review covers peer-reviewed empirical and review articles published from 2005 to 2026 in top aviation management, transportation, and tourism journals.

### 1.2 Review Objectives

1. To identify and categorize key service quality dimensions that influence passenger satisfaction in LCCs
2. To examine the mediating role of satisfaction in the service quality–loyalty relationship
3. To assess the moderating effects of price sensitivity, airline type, and passenger demographics
4. To identify theoretical frameworks applied in the aviation service quality literature
5. To map research trends and identify doctoral-level gaps for future inquiry

### Article 1 — Shen & Yahya (2021)

**A. Bibliographic Information**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Authors | Shen, C. & Yahya, Y. |
| Year | 2021 |
| Title | The Impact of Service Quality and Price on Passengers' Loyalty Towards Low-Cost Airlines: The Southeast Asia Perspective |
| Journal | **Journal of Air Transport Management** (Priority Level A) |
| Volume/Issue | Vol. 91, Article 101966 |
| DOI | 10.1016/j.jairtraman.2020.101966 |
| Index | Scopus Q1, SSCI |

**B. Research Variables**

- **Independent Variables:** Service quality (AIRQUAL: airline tangibles, terminal tangibles, personnel, empathy, image); price perception
- **Mediating Variable:** Passenger satisfaction
- **Dependent Variable:** Passenger loyalty
- **Control Variables:** Passenger demographics (age, gender, travel purpose)

**C. Theoretical Framework**
- AIRQUAL model (Ekiz et al., 2006; adapted from SERVQUAL)
- Expectancy disconfirmation paradigm (Oliver, 1980)
- Service-profit chain (Heskett et al., 1994)

**D. Research Methodology**
- Quantitative, cross-sectional survey design
- AIRQUAL-based structured questionnaire
- Survey conducted at Southeast Asian LCC hub airports

**E. Sample Characteristics**
- N = 200 LCC passengers
- Context: Southeast Asian low-cost carriers (AirAsia and regional equivalents)
- Sampling: Convenience sampling at airport departure gates

**F. Statistical Techniques**
- Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) via AMOS
- Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA)
- Bootstrap mediation analysis

**G. Key Findings**
1. All AIRQUAL service quality dimensions positively and significantly predict passenger satisfaction (β = 0.52, p < 0.001)
2. Price perception positively and significantly predicts passenger satisfaction (β = 0.31, p < 0.01)
3. Passenger satisfaction **fully mediates** both service quality → loyalty and price → loyalty paths
4. Neither service quality nor price directly predicts loyalty when satisfaction is controlled — satisfaction is the critical pivot
5. Personnel/empathy dimension has the strongest effect on satisfaction among all AIRQUAL dimensions

**H. Managerial Implications**
1. LCC managers must invest in frontline staff training and empathy-building — functional quality (people) matters as much as technical quality (aircraft, lounge)
2. Competitive pricing alone will not generate loyalty; airlines must ensure the price-value experience is satisfying to convert price-seekers into loyal customers
3. Loyalty programs should reward satisfaction-generating experiences, not just miles flown

**I. Study Limitations**
1. Small sample (n = 200) limits generalizability across the diverse SEA LCC market
2. Cross-sectional design cannot establish causal direction
3. Convenience sampling at hub airports overrepresents frequent travelers

**J. Future Research Directions**
1. Longitudinal studies tracking how LCC loyalty evolves across multiple trips
2. Multi-country comparative studies across AirAsia, Cebu Pacific, Lion Air, and Scoot
3. Integration of digital service quality (mobile apps, chatbots) as a new AIRQUAL dimension

### Article 2 — Forgas et al. (2010)

**A. Bibliographic Information**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Authors | Forgas, S., Moliner, M.A., Sánchez, J. & Palau, R. |
| Year | 2010 |
| Title | Antecedents of Airline Passenger Loyalty: Low-Cost Versus Traditional Airlines |
| Journal | **Journal of Air Transport Management** (Priority Level A) |
| Volume/Issue | Vol. 16(4), pp. 229–233 |
| DOI | 10.1016/j.jairtraman.2009.12.001 |
| Index | Scopus Q1, SSCI |

**B. Research Variables**

- **Independent Variables:** Perceived service quality, perceived value, customer satisfaction, trust
- **Mediating Variables:** Customer satisfaction, trust
- **Dependent Variable:** Passenger loyalty (attitudinal and behavioral)
- **Moderating Variable:** Airline type (LCC vs. FSC)

**C. Theoretical Framework**
- Relationship marketing theory
- Service quality → Satisfaction → Trust → Loyalty chain
- Value-based loyalty model

**D. Research Methodology**
- Quantitative, cross-sectional comparative design
- Survey at Spanish airports
- Separate structural model tested for LCC and FSC passengers

**E. Sample Characteristics**
- Total N = 470 (LCC: n = 237; FSC: n = 233)
- Spanish domestic and short-haul international passengers
- Sampling: Airport exit survey

**F. Statistical Techniques**
- Structural Equation Modeling (SEM)
- Multi-group analysis (LCC vs. FSC)
- Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA)

**G. Key Findings**
1. Satisfaction and trust are the two primary antecedents of loyalty for both airline types
2. Drivers of satisfaction differ: **price sensitivity dominates LCC satisfaction**; in-flight service quality dominates FSC satisfaction
3. LCC passengers exhibit significantly weaker overall loyalty, making retention structurally more difficult
4. Trust has an independent, direct effect on loyalty beyond satisfaction — particularly for FSC passengers
5. Perceived value (price–quality ratio) is a stronger predictor of satisfaction in LCCs than in FSCs

**H. Managerial Implications**
1. LCC loyalty programs should focus on value-for-money experiences and trust-building mechanisms
2. Service recovery protocols are critical for LCCs since their passengers have weaker baseline loyalty
3. Full-service carriers can leverage relationship-based trust strategies not available to LCCs

**I. Study Limitations**
1. Geographic limitation to Spain; results may differ in Asia-Pacific LCC markets where cultural factors vary
2. Single time-point measurement of loyalty does not capture behavioral loyalty over time
3. Satisfaction and trust treated as unidimensional constructs

**J. Future Research Directions**
1. Cross-cultural replication in Southeast Asian LCC markets where price sensitivity and loyalty dynamics differ
2. Longitudinal tracking of trust development differences across LCC and FSC relationships
3. Service failure disruption context: how does trust collapse differ between LCC and FSC passengers?

### Article 3 — Buaphiban & Truong (2017)

**A. Bibliographic Information**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Authors | Buaphiban, T. & Truong, D. |
| Year | 2017 |
| Title | Evaluation of Passengers' Buying Behaviors Toward Low Cost Carriers in Southeast Asia |
| Journal | **Journal of Air Transport Management** (Priority Level A) |
| Volume/Issue | Vol. 59, pp. 124–133 |
| DOI | 10.1016/j.jairtraman.2016.12.002 |
| Index | Scopus Q1, SSCI |

**B. Research Variables**

- **Independent Variables:** Attitude toward LCCs, subjective norms (social influence), perceived behavioral control
- **Mediating Variable:** Buying intention
- **Dependent Variable:** Actual buying behavior (LCC ticket purchase)
- **Contextual Variables:** Price sensitivity, gender, travel frequency

**C. Theoretical Framework**
- Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB; Ajzen, 1991)
- Decomposed TPB model for purchase behavior

**D. Research Methodology**
- Quantitative, cross-sectional survey at Bangkok airports (Suvarnabhumi and Don Mueang)
- Self-administered structured questionnaire
- Passengers who had purchased tickets for AirAsia, Thai Lion Air, or Nok Air

**E. Sample Characteristics**
- N = 781 passengers
- Context: Bangkok — Thailand's two major LCC hubs
- Mix of domestic and international LCC travelers

**F. Statistical Techniques**
- Structural Equation Modeling (SEM)
- Path analysis within TPB framework
- Mediation testing (intention as mediator)

**G. Key Findings**
1. Attitudes and subjective norms (social approval) have significant **indirect** positive effects on actual buying behavior through buying intention
2. Perceived behavioral control has a **direct** effect on actual behavior beyond its effect through intention
3. Price sensitivity is the primary attitudinal driver — Thai LCC passengers are predominantly price-motivated
4. Social influence (family/friend recommendations) plays a significant role, particularly for first-time LCC users
5. Gender moderates the subjective norms pathway — women are more influenced by social norms than men

**H. Managerial Implications**
1. LCC marketing should leverage social proof and word-of-mouth to activate subjective norm pathways
2. Simplifying the booking process strengthens perceived behavioral control and actual purchase
3. Price transparency and clear value-for-money communication reinforce positive attitudes

**I. Study Limitations**
1. Survey at only two Bangkok airports — may not represent rural or smaller-city LCC passengers
2. TPB does not include post-purchase variables (satisfaction, loyalty)
3. Cross-sectional; actual buying behavior was self-reported, not verified from purchase records

**J. Future Research Directions**
1. Longitudinal extension tracking whether intended LCC purchases actualize over time
2. Post-purchase satisfaction integrated into TPB to test full pre-to-post purchase LCC adoption model
3. Comparative study across Bangkok, Kuala Lumpur, and Jakarta to identify cultural moderators

### Article 4 — Pan & Truong (2018)

**A. Bibliographic Information**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Authors | Pan, J.Y. & Truong, D. |
| Year | 2018 |
| Title | Passengers' Intentions to Use Low-Cost Carriers: An Extended Theory of Planned Behavior Model |
| Journal | **Journal of Air Transport Management** (Priority Level A) |
| Volume/Issue | Vol. 69, pp. 38–48 |
| DOI | 10.1016/j.jairtraman.2017.12.002 |
| Index | Scopus Q1, SSCI |

**B. Research Variables**

- **Independent Variables:** Attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioral control, price, service quality, airport accessibility, uncertainty avoidance, technology self-efficacy
- **Dependent Variable:** Intention to use LCC
- **Moderating Variables:** Uncertainty avoidance (Hofstede), technology self-efficacy

**C. Theoretical Framework**
- Extended Theory of Planned Behavior (Ajzen, 1991)
- Hofstede's cultural dimensions (uncertainty avoidance)
- Technology self-efficacy theory (Bandura, 1977)

**D. Research Methodology**
- Quantitative, cross-sectional survey at two major Chinese airports
- Extended TPB questionnaire with additional cultural and technology variables

**E. Sample Characteristics**
- N = 596 passengers with prior LCC flight experience
- Context: Chinese domestic LCC passengers
- Broad age and income range

**F. Statistical Techniques**
- Structural Equation Modeling (SEM)
- Hierarchical regression for sequential TPB extensions
- Moderation analysis for uncertainty avoidance and technology self-efficacy

**G. Key Findings**
1. **Price is the single strongest determinant** of LCC use intention (β = 0.41, p < 0.001)
2. Service quality significantly influences intentions independently of price
3. Uncertainty avoidance negatively moderates the price–intention relationship — culturally risk-averse passengers are less willing to sacrifice service quality for low prices
4. Technology self-efficacy positively moderates online booking adoption
5. Perceived behavioral control and flight frequency are NOT significant — challenging standard TPB predictions in LCC contexts

**H. Managerial Implications**
1. Price transparency combined with basic service quality communication maximizes LCC adoption
2. Online booking platforms must be designed for ease of use to reduce technology barriers for older passengers
3. In high uncertainty-avoidance markets, LCCs should communicate safety and reliability more prominently

**I. Study Limitations**
1. Chinese context may not generalize to Southeast Asian passengers (different uncertainty avoidance levels)
2. Intentions may not translate to actual behavior — no booking data validation
3. Cross-sectional design

**J. Future Research Directions**
1. Cross-cultural replication across Thailand, Indonesia, Vietnam to test uncertainty avoidance moderation
2. Tracking whether stated intentions translate to actual LCC bookings (behavioral validation)
3. Digital service quality as an independent construct separate from overall service quality

### Article 5 — Truong, Pan & Buaphiban (2020)

**A. Bibliographic Information**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Authors | Truong, D., Pan, J.Y. & Buaphiban, T. |
| Year | 2020 |
| Title | Low Cost Carriers in Southeast Asia: How Does Ticket Price Change the Way Passengers Make Their Airline Selection? |
| Journal | **Journal of Air Transport Management** (Priority Level A) |
| Volume/Issue | Vol. 86(C), Article 101833 |
| DOI | 10.1016/j.jairtraman.2020.101833 |
| Index | Scopus Q1, SSCI |

**B. Research Variables**

- **Independent Variables:** Attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioral control
- **Moderating Variable:** Ticket price (continuous)
- **Dependent Variable:** Behavioral intention to select LCC
- **Contextual Variables:** Airline brand (AirAsia, Thai Lion Air, Nok Air), route type

**C. Theoretical Framework**
- Theory of Planned Behavior (Ajzen, 1991)
- Price moderation within the attitude–intention pathway
- Prospect theory (loss aversion as price increases)

**D. Research Methodology**
- Bangkok airport survey (same base dataset as Buaphiban & Truong, 2017; N = 781)
- Price tested as a continuous moderating variable with interaction terms
- Johnson–Neyman technique for identifying significance regions

**E. Sample Characteristics**
- N = 781 passengers at Suvarnabhumi and Don Mueang airports
- AirAsia, Thai Lion Air, Nok Air passengers
- Mix of domestic and short-haul international travelers

**F. Statistical Techniques**
- SEM with moderation testing
- Johnson–Neyman technique
- Multi-group comparison at different price points

**G. Key Findings**
1. **At lower price points:** attitude toward the airline is the primary driver of selection intention
2. **As price increases:** subjective norms (social approval) become more influential — passengers seek peer validation for higher-cost LCC purchases
3. Price significantly moderates the attitude→intention pathway (interaction β = −0.18, p < 0.05)
4. Price sensitivity is the primary selection criterion for SEA LCC passengers overall
5. AirAsia passengers show higher brand attitude effects than Thai Lion Air or Nok Air at all price points

**H. Managerial Implications**
1. At low-fare flash sales: emphasize individual attitude cues (comfort, reliability)
2. At standard/high fares: activate social proof and peer testimonials
3. Airlines with stronger brand equity can sustain higher prices without losing the attitude-driven purchase

**I. Study Limitations**
1. Moderation used retrospective self-reported price recall, not actual transaction data
2. Limited to Bangkok — may not represent secondary city LCC passengers
3. Temporal effects (post-COVID price changes) not captured

**J. Future Research Directions**
1. Real transaction data experiment testing price thresholds in actual booking engines
2. Post-COVID fare recovery context: do price sensitivity thresholds shift after travel scarcity?
3. Fare framing experiments (all-in vs. unbundled pricing) and moderation of transparency on attitude–intention

### Article 6 — Akamavi et al. (2015)

**A. Bibliographic Information**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Authors | Akamavi, R.K., Mohamed, E., Pellmann, K. & Xu, Y. |
| Year | 2015 |
| Title | Key Determinants of Passenger Loyalty in the Low-Cost Airline Business |
| Journal | **Tourism Management** (Priority Level A) |
| Volume/Issue | Vol. 46, pp. 528–545 |
| DOI | 10.1016/j.tourman.2014.07.010 |
| Index | SSCI, Scopus Q1 |

**B. Research Variables**

- **Independent Variables:** Service employee self-efficacy, service recovery quality, perceived justice (distributive, procedural, interactional), passenger trust
- **Mediating Variables:** Passenger satisfaction, trust
- **Dependent Variable:** Passenger loyalty (attitudinal and behavioral)
- **Context Variable:** Prior service failure experience

**C. Theoretical Framework**
- Service recovery theory (Smith et al., 1999)
- Justice theory / equity theory (Adams, 1965)
- Social exchange theory
- Service employee self-efficacy (Bandura, 1977)

**D. Research Methodology**
- Quantitative, cross-sectional survey
- Passengers who experienced a service failure on an LCC within the past 12 months
- Survey at two major UK airports

**E. Sample Characteristics**
- N = 286 LCC passengers post-service-failure
- Context: UK LCC market (Ryanair, easyJet)
- Purposive sampling (service failure experience required)

**F. Statistical Techniques**
- Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) via LISREL
- Mediation analysis
- Cronbach's α, AVE, and CR for reliability and validity

**G. Key Findings**
1. **Service employee self-efficacy** is the single strongest predictor of post-failure passenger satisfaction (β = 0.38)
2. Service recovery quality (perceived justice) directly and positively affects trust (β = 0.44) and satisfaction (β = 0.29)
3. Passenger satisfaction — not price — is the primary driver of loyalty enhancement post-recovery
4. Trust has an independent direct effect on loyalty beyond its indirect path through satisfaction
5. **Interactional justice** (how staff treated the passenger) has the strongest effect among three justice dimensions

**H. Managerial Implications**
1. LCC operators should invest in frontline staff empowerment — self-efficacious employees resolve failures faster and restore satisfaction more effectively
2. Recovery protocols must address all three justice dimensions: fair compensation (distributive), fair process (procedural), respectful communication (interactional)
3. Trust-building after recovery events is as important as satisfaction restoration

**I. Study Limitations**
1. UK-centric; cultural responses to service failure differ in face-saving Southeast Asian cultures
2. Purposive sampling of failure-experienced passengers creates negative-experience selection bias
3. Self-reported loyalty measures may not predict actual repurchase behavior

**J. Future Research Directions**
1. Cross-cultural comparison of service recovery in Asian LCC contexts (face-saving norms have different interactional justice expectations)
2. Longitudinal study: does recovered trust return to pre-failure levels or remain structurally lower?
3. Digital recovery channels (chatbot compensation, app reimbursement) — do they match in-person recovery effectiveness?

### Article 7 — Rajaguru (2016)

**A. Bibliographic Information**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Author | Rajaguru, R. |
| Year | 2016 |
| Title | Role of Value for Money and Service Quality on Behavioural Intention: A Study of Full Service and Low Cost Airlines |
| Journal | **Journal of Air Transport Management** (Priority Level A) |
| Volume/Issue | Vol. 53, pp. 114–122 |
| DOI | 10.1016/j.jairtraman.2016.02.008 |
| Index | Scopus Q1, SSCI |

**B. Research Variables**

- **Independent Variables:** Value for money; service quality (tangibles, reliability, assurance, empathy, responsiveness)
- **Mediating Variable:** Customer satisfaction
- **Dependent Variable:** Behavioral intention (repurchase, recommendation)
- **Moderating Variable:** Airline type (LCC vs. FSC)

**C. Theoretical Framework**
- Means-end chain theory (Gutman, 1982)
- SERVQUAL model (Parasuraman et al., 1988)
- Expectancy disconfirmation theory (Oliver, 1980)

**D. Research Methodology**
- Quantitative, cross-sectional comparative survey
- Australian domestic airline passengers (LCC vs. FSC)
- SERVQUAL-based questionnaire with value-for-money scale

**E. Sample Characteristics**
- N = 297 domestic passengers (LCC: n = 148; FSC: n = 149)
- Australian airports; convenience sampling

**F. Statistical Techniques**
- Moderated regression analysis
- SEM-based path analysis
- Airline type as moderator (multi-group testing)

**G. Key Findings**
1. Value for money and service quality are both significant predictors of satisfaction and behavioral intention
2. **Personnel quality has a significantly weaker effect** on satisfaction for LCC vs. FSC passengers
3. Satisfaction has a **significantly stronger effect** on repurchase intention for LCC passengers than FSC passengers
4. Once an LCC passenger is satisfied, they are more committed to returning — yet satisfaction is harder to achieve in the first place
5. The means-end chain holds: Value for money → Satisfaction → Behavioral intention (full mediation confirmed)

**H. Managerial Implications**
1. LCCs should focus marketing on value-for-money positioning rather than matching FSC service quality
2. Personnel training should prioritize efficiency (processing correctly) over personalized service
3. The higher loyalty reward from LCC satisfaction makes every satisfaction-generating investment more valuable per dollar spent

**I. Study Limitations**
1. Australian mature LCC market — findings may differ in developing aviation markets
2. Cross-sectional design
3. Personnel quality treated as unidimensional — does not distinguish check-in, cabin crew, and customer service

**J. Future Research Directions**
1. Replication in Asia-Pacific LCC markets with different cultural service quality norms
2. Longitudinal analysis of how value-for-money perceptions change as LCC dynamic pricing evolves
3. Disaggregate personnel quality into pre-flight, in-flight, and post-flight staff contact points

### Article 8 — Shah et al. (2020)

**A. Bibliographic Information**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Authors | Shah, F.T., Syed, Z., Imam, A. & Raza, A. |
| Year | 2020 |
| Title | The Impact of Airline Service Quality on Passengers' Behavioral Intentions Using Passenger Satisfaction as a Mediator |
| Journal | **Journal of Air Transport Management** (Priority Level A) |
| Volume/Issue | Vol. 85, Article 101815 |
| DOI | 10.1016/j.jairtraman.2019.101815 |
| Index | Scopus Q1, SSCI |

**B. Research Variables**

- **Independent Variables:** Tangibles, reliability, assurance, empathy (SERVQUAL sub-dimensions)
- **Mediating Variable:** Passenger satisfaction
- **Dependent Variable:** Behavioral intentions (repurchase, positive word-of-mouth)
- **Control Variables:** Flight class, travel purpose, nationality

**C. Theoretical Framework**
- SERVQUAL model (Parasuraman et al., 1988)
- Expectancy disconfirmation theory (Oliver, 1980)
- Theory of Reasoned Action (Fishbein & Ajzen, 1975)

**D. Research Methodology**
- Quantitative, cross-sectional airport exit survey
- Adapted 22-item SERVQUAL instrument
- Data collected from Pakistan International Airlines passengers

**E. Sample Characteristics**
- N = 302 international airline passengers
- Mix of business and economy class
- Airport exit survey methodology

**F. Statistical Techniques**
- Structural Equation Modeling (SEM via AMOS)
- Bootstrap mediation analysis (5,000 iterations)
- Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA)

**G. Key Findings**
1. Passenger satisfaction **fully mediates** all four service quality dimensions → behavioral intentions
2. **Assurance** (safety communication, staff knowledge) has the strongest indirect effect through satisfaction
3. **Empathy** has the second strongest effect — personalized attention drives satisfaction significantly
4. Tangibles (aircraft condition, food) have the weakest effect — passengers adapt expectations for physical elements
5. Service quality has no significant **direct** effect on behavioral intentions when satisfaction is controlled

**H. Managerial Implications**
1. Airlines must treat satisfaction as the gateway metric — assurance and empathy investment directly generates the satisfaction that produces loyalty behaviors
2. Physical product improvements (new aircraft, premium meals) have diminishing returns compared to interpersonal service improvements
3. Separate SERVQUAL tracking by dimension identifies the weakest link for each passenger segment

**I. Study Limitations**
1. Single-airline context (PIA) limits generalizability to LCCs and other cultural contexts
2. Responsiveness excluded due to multicollinearity — potentially important omission
3. Cross-sectional; causality cannot be established

**J. Future Research Directions**
1. Multi-airline comparison testing whether SERVQUAL mediation effects differ between LCCs and FSCs
2. Post-pandemic adaptation: has the relative importance of assurance (safety) vs. empathy shifted?
3. Digital service quality dimensions (app reliability, chatbot responsiveness) should be integrated into updated SERVQUAL frameworks

### Article 9 — Hanaoka et al. (2014)

**A. Bibliographic Information**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Authors | Hanaoka, S., Takebayashi, M., Ishikura, T. & Saraswati, B. |
| Year | 2014 |
| Title | Low Cost Carriers Versus Full Service Carriers in ASEAN: The Impact of Liberalization Policy on Competition |
| Journal | **Journal of Air Transport Management** (Priority Level A) |
| Volume/Issue | Vol. 40, pp. 96–105 |
| DOI | 10.1016/j.jairtraman.2014.06.005 |
| Index | Scopus Q1, SSCI |

**B. Research Variables**

- **Independent Variables:** ASEAN aviation liberalization policy, LCC network expansion, route density
- **Dependent Variables:** Airfare levels, passenger volumes, LCC vs. FSC market share
- **Contextual Variables:** Secondary airport usage, route length, bilateral air service agreements

**C. Theoretical Framework**
- Industrial organization theory (competition dynamics)
- Airline network theory
- ASEAN aviation liberalization framework (ASAM/Open Sky)

**D. Research Methodology**
- Quantitative scenario-based policy modeling
- Network analysis under multiple liberalization scenarios
- Route-level fare and demand simulation

**E. Sample Characteristics**
- Secondary data: route-level aviation statistics across 10 ASEAN nations
- Airlines modeled: AirAsia, Lion Air, Cebu Pacific, Scoot + major FSCs
- All intra-ASEAN international routes

**F. Statistical Techniques**
- Gravity model for demand estimation
- Scenario analysis (full ASAM vs. bilateral vs. status quo)
- Network graph analysis of route connectivity

**G. Key Findings**
1. Under full ASAM liberalization, LCCs would increase intra-ASEAN passengers by **35–60%** on dense short-haul corridors
2. Airfares on LCC-operated routes projected to decrease **20–40%** under full liberalization
3. LCCs will structurally displace FSCs on routes under 3 hours — FSCs retain advantage only on long-haul and premium segments
4. Secondary airport development is critical — major hubs become capacity-constrained as LCC demand grows
5. Regional benefits are uneven: Thailand, Indonesia, and Malaysia benefit most; smaller nations (Laos, Myanmar, Cambodia) gain less

**H. Managerial Implications**
1. Airport authorities in secondary cities should plan capacity expansion in anticipation of LCC growth
2. FSC strategy must pivot to differentiation on premium and long-haul routes where LCC competition is structurally weaker
3. National regulators should accelerate bilateral-to-multilateral agreement conversion to maximize consumer benefit

**I. Study Limitations**
1. Gravity model assumes rational market clearing — ignores airline loyalty, brand preference, and service quality heterogeneity
2. LCC cost structures assumed constant — no modeling of fuel price volatility or infrastructure cost differences
3. COVID-19 (post-publication) fundamentally disrupted all ASEAN aviation integration assumptions

**J. Future Research Directions**
1. Post-COVID recalibration of ASEAN aviation liberalization impact modeling
2. Integration of sustainability mandates (carbon taxes, SAF) into LCC competitiveness modeling
3. Service quality as a competitive differentiator in liberalized markets — when price parity is achieved, does service quality drive market share?

## 5. Emerging Research Themes

### Theme 1: Digital Service Quality as a Distinct Dimension

The rapid proliferation of mobile check-in, AI chatbots, and self-service kiosks has created a distinct digital service quality (DSQ) layer not captured by SERVQUAL's five traditional dimensions. TAM-based studies (Taufik & Hanafiah, 2019; Wongyai et al., 2024) show that perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness of digital touchpoints predict adoption and satisfaction independently of face-to-face service quality. The bibliometric analysis by Bakır et al. (2025) confirms this as the fastest-growing sub-cluster in airline SQ research.

### Theme 2: Post-COVID Safety as a Permanent Quality Dimension

Hassan & Salem (2022) demonstrate that COVID-19 safety protocols (distancing, visible cleaning, mask enforcement) significantly enhanced perceived quality, satisfaction, and loyalty. As aviation normalizes post-COVID, a critical question is whether **biohygiene quality** becomes a permanent sixth SERVQUAL dimension or reverts to a base-rate expectation that produces dissatisfaction only when absent.

### Theme 3: Personality-Informed Service Personalization

Leon & Dixon (2023) introduce Big Five personality traits as predictors of airline satisfaction — a novel approach opening the door to AI-driven service personalization systems that dynamically adjust in-flight service based on inferred personality profiles. Agreeable and open passengers respond positively to standard service; extraverted passengers require elevated personalization.

### Theme 4: UGC and Sentiment Analysis for Real-Time SQ Monitoring

The fastest-growing bibliometric sub-cluster (Bakır et al., 2025) is the use of user-generated content (TripAdvisor, Google reviews, social media) and NLP/sentiment analysis to monitor service quality gaps in near-real-time — replacing or augmenting traditional survey-based SERVQUAL measurement. This represents a fundamental methodological shift from retrospective survey data to continuous real-time monitoring.

### Theme 5: Hybrid LCC Business Model Evolution

The LCC model is evolving — ultra-LCCs (zero-frills), hybrid carriers (LCC-plus), and long-haul LCCs (AirAsia X, Scoot) represent distinct service quality propositions. The Research in Transportation Business & Management (2025) review notes that academic attention is **declining despite growing LCC market share** — a growing gap between industry evolution and academic research productivity.

## 6. Underexplored Research Areas

Based on the literature synthesis, the following areas are significantly underexplored:

1. **Rural and Tier-2 City LCC Passengers in Southeast Asia:** Most studies concentrate on major hub airports (Bangkok, Kuala Lumpur, Jakarta). Secondary cities (Chiang Mai, Kota Kinabalu, Surabaya) represent distinct passenger profiles that may exhibit different satisfaction and loyalty dynamics.

2. **Cross-Generational Service Quality Preferences:** The literature lacks systematic analysis of how Millennial vs. Gen Z vs. Baby Boomer passengers in Southeast Asia evaluate LCC service quality — an important commercial segmentation gap.

3. **Sustainability and Green Aviation Service Quality:** Environmental sustainability (carbon offsets, SAF communication, zero-waste practices) as a dimension of LCC service quality is virtually absent from reviewed literature, despite growing passenger environmental awareness.

4. **LCC Service Quality for Special Needs Passengers:** Accessibility-related service quality for passengers with reduced mobility, sensory disabilities, or dietary requirements in the LCC context is rarely studied.

5. **Intermodal Integration Quality:** As LCC passengers increasingly combine air travel with ground transport (rail, bus, ride-hailing), the quality of the seamless journey determines overall satisfaction and loyalty — yet no study models this holistically.

6. **Trust in AI-Mediated Airline Services:** As AI chatbots replace human agents for complaint handling and rebooking, the trust dynamics and satisfaction implications for LCC passengers are almost entirely unstudied.

7. **Long-Haul LCCs in Southeast Asia:** Most research focuses on short-haul LCCs; AirAsia X and Scoot's long-haul operations present fundamentally different service quality requirements and satisfaction drivers warranting dedicated study.

8. **LCC Post-Recovery Behavioral Intentions (Post-COVID):** How the COVID-19 pandemic altered the structural relationship between satisfaction and loyalty in LCCs — particularly for passengers who experienced flight cancellations or voucher disputes — remains an open question.

                        ↓

                        ↓
                  Passenger Loyalty

**Methodological approach:** CFA to validate the integrated scale; SEM to test the loyalty model

```
Service Quality
      ↓           ← Moderating: Power Distance

**Geographic scope:** Thailand, Vietnam, Indonesia, Malaysia

**Rationale:** A conceptual model tracing how LCC passengers' trust and loyalty trajectories evolve after disruptive events (flight cancellations, pandemics, aircraft incidents). Integrates service recovery theory, justice theory, and trust-commitment theory.

**Theoretical anchors:** Service recovery theory + EDT + Trust-commitment theory  
**Key extension:** Digital recovery channels (app, chatbot) vs. in-person recovery effectiveness comparison

### Framework 4: Sustainability-Extended LCC Value Proposition Model

Social Value (brand status/image)
```

**Theoretical anchors:** Multi-dimensional value theory + Green marketing theory + Customer loyalty model

---

### Gap 1: Absence of a Validated LCC Service Quality Scale for Southeast Asia

**Nature of gap:** No single validated, multi-dimensional service quality scale has been developed specifically for the Southeast Asian LCC market. Existing instruments (SERVQUAL, AIRQUAL) were developed in Western or general Asian contexts and may not capture culturally-specific service quality constructs (e.g., smiling culture, face-saving in complaint handling, language-preference in service) relevant to Thai, Indonesian, or Vietnamese passengers.

**Doctoral contribution:** Development and psychometric validation of a Southeast Asia-specific LCC service quality scale — tentatively labeled **SEAQUAL** — integrating cultural service dimensions alongside standard AIRQUAL dimensions, validated through multi-country EFA/CFA across Thailand, Malaysia, Vietnam, and Indonesia.

**Expected impact:** Provides a standardized measurement instrument for 15–20 years of future LCC service quality research in the region; addresses the methodological fragmentation identified across reviewed studies.

---

### Gap 2: Longitudinal Study of LCC Loyalty Formation

**Nature of gap:** All reviewed studies are cross-sectional, providing a single-point snapshot of satisfaction and loyalty. No longitudinal study tracks how an individual passenger's satisfaction and loyalty to a Southeast Asian LCC evolve across multiple flight experiences.

**Doctoral contribution:** A panel study (minimum 3 time points, 12-month window) tracking the same passengers across repeated LCC flights, measuring how satisfaction antecedents and loyalty outcomes evolve — and identifying **threshold satisfaction levels** that predict long-term loyalty commitment.

**Expected impact:** Transforms the theoretical understanding of LCC loyalty from a static snapshot to a dynamic trajectory; enables airlines to design loyalty programs with data-driven intervention timing.

---

### Gap 3: Digital vs. Physical Service Quality Effects on LCC Loyalty in Post-COVID Asia

**Nature of gap:** The bifurcation of airline service into physical (cabin, staff, food) and digital (app, kiosk, chatbot) channels creates a dual service quality structure not captured in any existing Southeast Asian LCC study. No study has simultaneously modeled both digital and physical SQ effects on loyalty in this region.

**Doctoral contribution:** A dual-channel LCC service quality model tested in Thailand and Malaysia — producing the first empirical evidence on the **relative weight of digital vs. physical SQ** dimensions in driving LCC loyalty in post-COVID Southeast Asia.

**Expected impact:** Directly informs airline digital investment decisions; provides priority rankings for technology vs. human service investment under constrained LCC budgets.

---

### Gap 4: Sustainability as a Loyalty Driver in Southeast Asian LCCs

**Nature of gap:** Environmental sustainability is entirely absent from the Southeast Asian LCC service quality literature. With increasing regional ESG awareness among young travelers and ICAO's CORSIA mandates, LCCs' green performance will increasingly affect brand loyalty.

**Doctoral contribution:** First systematic empirical study of sustainability perception → brand image → loyalty chain in Southeast Asian LCCs, providing the theoretical foundation for green positioning strategies for AirAsia, VietJet, and Nok Air.

**Expected impact:** Opens a new sub-field of sustainable aviation consumer behavior in the ASEAN context; contributes to both aviation management and sustainability management theory.

---

### Gap 5: Aviation Policy and Service Quality Incentives in the ASEAN Open Sky Framework

**Nature of gap:** Hanaoka et al. (2014) modeled liberalization effects on competition and pricing but not on service quality incentives. No study has tested whether ASEAN Open Sky policy **increases or decreases** LCC service quality investment — a critical policy question for regional aviation authorities.

**Doctoral contribution:** A mixed-methods policy analysis combining qualitative stakeholder interviews (airline managers, civil aviation authorities, ASEAN regulators) with quantitative service quality tracking data to assess whether liberalization drives or undermines LCC service quality standards.

**Expected impact:** Directly informs ASEAN aviation policy at the regional level; provides the first empirical evidence on the liberalization–service quality relationship in a developing-region LCC market.
